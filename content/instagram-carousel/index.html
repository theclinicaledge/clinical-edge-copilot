Early Warning Signals Most Nurses Miss

These don't always look dramatic at the bedside.

01

Altered mental status before vitals change

Restlessness, confusion, or new agitation often precede objective deterioration by 30+ minutes.

02

Narrowing pulse pressure

A BP of 110/90 is different from 110/70. The difference matters more than the systolic alone.

03

Skin perfusion changes

Mottling, cool extremities, or prolonged cap refill (>3 sec) signals redistributed circulation.

04

Increased work of breathing without low SpO₂

The patient compensating is maintaining O₂ — for now. Watch the effort, not just the number.

What This Looks Like at the Bedside

Here's how Clinical Edge Copilot reasons through a real case:

Nurse's Input

"67yo post-op day 1 abdominal surgery. HR 102, BP 104/88, RR 22, SpO2 95% on 2L NC. Patient says 'I just feel off.' Urine output 15 mL/hr past 2 hours. Temp 37.8."

Urgency Level

HIGH — Early Sepsis Picture

What This Could Be

Post-op sepsis / anastomotic leak. Compensated shock with narrowing pulse pressure (PP = 16), oliguria, and subjective complaint preceding objective collapse.

Next Action

Notify surgical team now. Sepsis workup: lactate, CBC, BMP, blood cultures × 2. IV fluid bolus. Upgrade monitoring.
